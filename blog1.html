Understanding Common Medications

April 10, 2025

Have you heard of rheumatic heart disease (RHD)?

No? You’re not alone...

        While awareness of RHD is growing globally, it remains a serious but often overlooked public health issue in Bangladesh, particularly in rural and low-income communities.
        
        So, why is RHD still affecting thousands in Bangladesh every year? Let’s understand what RHD is, how people get it, and why it's still a concern.
        
        What is RHD?
        RHD begins with an infection caused by group A streptococcus (Strep A) — a bacteria that commonly infects the throat (strep throat) or skin. These infections are widespread and often treatable with antibiotics if detected early.
        
        However, sometimes the immune system becomes confused. Instead of only attacking the bacteria, it mistakenly attacks the body’s own tissues — especially the heart — in an autoimmune response known as acute rheumatic fever (ARF).
        
        This inflammation can cause permanent damage to the heart valves, leading to rheumatic heart disease (RHD) — a lifelong condition that affects the heart's ability to function properly. Unfortunately, RHD has no cure, and valve damage is irreversible.
        
        RHD in Bangladesh
        RHD continues to be a major public health concern in Bangladesh, especially in children and young adults.
        
        Despite medical advancements, thousands of new RHD cases are diagnosed each year, especially in underserved rural communities. According to the World Heart Federation, Bangladesh remains among the countries with a high burden of RHD, with many cases going undetected or untreated due to gaps in healthcare infrastructure.
        
        Why is RHD still common in Bangladesh?
        RHD is closely linked to poverty and limited access to healthcare. In many parts of Bangladesh, particularly in rural or densely populated areas, families face challenges such as:
        
        Overcrowded living conditions
        
        Poor sanitation
        
        Limited access to clean water
        
        Inadequate healthcare facilities
        
        Lack of awareness about the symptoms and dangers of strep infections
        
        When strep throat or skin infections aren’t treated in time — either due to a lack of medical services or cultural and financial barriers — the risk of developing rheumatic fever and then RHD increases drastically.
        
        Barriers to healthcare in Bangladesh
        Accessing quality healthcare remains a challenge for many in Bangladesh. Some of the main barriers include:
        
        Financial hardship
        
        Long travel distances to clinics or hospitals
        
        Shortage of trained healthcare providers in remote areas
        
        Limited awareness about the importance of treating sore throats
        
        Cultural stigma or fear surrounding hospitals or medical treatment
        
        In addition, many people do not return for follow-up care or long-term treatment, which is essential for managing RHD.
        
        What can be done to prevent RHD?
        RHD is preventable — especially if Strep A infections are treated early with a simple course of antibiotics. Long-term prevention includes:
        
        Awareness campaigns about sore throat treatment
        
        Improved primary healthcare access in rural areas
        
        Training for community health workers
        
        Echocardiographic screening programs
        
        Regular penicillin injections for those already diagnosed to prevent further damage
        
        What does the future hold for Bangladesh?
        Research and public health efforts are beginning to focus more on RHD prevention and control in Bangladesh. Some goals for the future include:
        
        Developing a Strep A vaccine
        
        Introducing community-based screening programs
        
        Improving data collection and tracking of RHD cases
        
        Ensuring free or low-cost treatment in rural clinics
        
        Integrating RHD awareness into school health programs
        
        Projects & Awareness Initiatives
        Bangladesh has potential to learn from successful RHD control programs in other countries and adapt them locally. Efforts could include:
        
        School-based education about sore throat and hygiene
        
        Training village doctors and pharmacists
        
        Expanding outreach via mobile health clinics
        
        Collaborating with international health bodies like WHO and the World Heart Federation
        
        Want to learn more about RHD in Bangladesh?
        Visit the World Heart Federation website
        
        Explore resources on rheumatic heart disease prevention
        
        Support local organizations working in rural healthcare
        
        Encourage routine check-ups for children with recurring sore throats
        
        Together, with awareness, access, and action, Bangladesh can significantly reduce the burden of RHD — and protect the hearts of future generations. ❤️
